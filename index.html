Sexual Assaults in Ride-Hailing Services in Chicago: infrequent but shocking

How do these services stack up against other forms of transportation?

By Corinne Osnos

She walked up to his silver Nissan Sentra, asking her Uber driver to wait while she went back inside to take another quick shot of the liquor she’d been drinking that June evening.

Her driver, Angel Munoz, picked up his passenger from the South Side, where former President Barack Obama once “had his share of troubles catching cabs,” his wife, Michelle, told People magazine in 2014.

The woman, who looked to be in her early 20s, spoke animatedly to Munoz and the other passengers in the ride-share, even breaking into a rap at one point.

But after the others were dropped off at their destinations, she fell “dead asleep” in the backseat, said Munoz.

A few weeks later Munoz heard the news report a Chicago woman had accused a Lyft driver of kidnapping and sexually assaulting her. Munoz was instantly transported back to that evening in late June.

“She was knocked out. I called her name, shook her lightly, then harder, but she was still completely non-responsive,” said Munoz, 42, who lives in Humboldt Park and is the father of two daughters.

He’d been driving for Uber, Lyft’s predecessor and primary competitor, for five months at the time.

No mode of transportation is safe 100 percent of the time.

Ride-hailing services are ubiquitous, particularly for the large number of millennials opting to live in urban areas like Chicago.

There are many benefits to these services, most notably the ease in which passengers can request, direct and pay for rides.

According to an analysis of Chicago Police Department data, there have been nine reports of sexual assault in ride-hailing services since Uber’s arrival in Chicago in 2011; Lyft followed in 2013. When these incidents do occur, however, they receive disproportionate coverage in the media.

The number of sexual assaults reported in taxicabs is also considerably low, ranging between one and five per year since 2012. So far in 2017, only one incident has been reported.

The Chicago Police Department defines criminal sexual assault as “any sexual act directed against another person, forcibly and/or against that person's will or not forcibly or against the person's will in instances where the victim is incapable of giving consent.”

The majority of sexual offenses on the train, however, are incidents of public indecency, a crime violating the sanctity of a public space, but is far less serious than sexual assault.

Out of 134 total incidents on the train in the past five years, 74 have been reports of public indecency.

One reason stories of sexual assaults in ride-hailing services are over-reported is that they tend to shock the conscience, despite the infrequency with which they occur. The victims are overwhelmingly young, intoxicated women, and the accusations, severe.

The data suggest the number of sexual assaults in ride-hailing services is increasing, but the frequency is still considerably lower than mass transit, including CTA buses, bus stops, platforms, and trains, along with taxicabs. In 2015, three sexual assaults were reported during ride services.

In 2016, four incidents were reported, and halfway through 2017, two have been reported.

Sexual assaults in ride-sharing services happen in Chicago, but not as often as media coverage suggests.

In the past five years, 5,456 sexual offenses occurred in Chicago–7.6 percent related to transit. Consistently, the highest rate of sexual assaults occurs on CTA trains, with 26 incidents in 2017 to date.

In March 2014, Vy Ho sued Uber Technologies and Jigneshkumar Patel in Cook County Court, claiming her driver, Patel, propositioned her for sex. When Ho refused, she told authorities Patel groped her breast, legs and groin, only letting her out of the car when she threatened to call the police.

According to their codes of ethics, both Uber and Lyft perform background checks on all their drivers, and track cars once a driver opts to take a ride.

“Uber does not disclose that drivers can, in fact, turn this function off, rendering them untrackable,” Ms. Ho told Courthouse News in 2014.

The case settled for an undisclosed amount, said Kristi Browne, Ho’s attorney.

In the most recent case in Chicago, a 25-year-old woman fell asleep shortly after getting in her Lyft vehicle following a night of drinking in River North with friends, according to The Chicago Tribune. The victim told authorities her Lyft driver drove into an alley and tied her up with zip-ties before raping her at knifepoint.

NBC reported the driver in question, Angelo McCoy, is being held on bail for $90,000, has been charged with aggravated criminal sexual assault, aggravated kidnapping, armed robbery and unlawful restraint.

In a 2014 report, Uber used Chicago as a case study, arguing its services had democratized transportation in the city.

The features that make ride-hailing services appealing also create a false sense of security. These services normalize entering a stranger’s car, an act once considered dangerous.

In March 2015, Uber responded to stories of misconduct by their drivers in the media, most notably, a case of rape in New Delhi that caused the service to be banned.

The company announced a series of new initiatives to help passengers feel safer, including a new internal safety advisory board, along with a quality assurance program and incident response teams.

"We are committed to ensuring Uber is the safest way to get around a city," wrote Phillip Cardenas, Uber’s Head of Global Safety, reaffirming the company’s mission in a blog post.

Angel Munoz said it took 20 minutes to awaken his passed-out-drunk passenger that evening in June 2017.

“Finally, I had to go around to the outside of the car, open her door and sit her up in back seat, and give her a little smack in the face just to wake her up out of it,” he said.

Munoz said he was scared.

He didn’t know if he should call the police. He didn’t want her to wake up at the wrong moment and think he was trying to take advantage of her—luckily, she didn’t.

He said, “Thank god it was me that had her in the car and not somebody else.”
